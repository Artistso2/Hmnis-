## What is the Geneva Convention?

The **Geneva Conventions** are a series of four international treaties (established in 1949) and three subsequent additional protocols. Together, they form the bedrock of **International Humanitarian Law (IHL)**—commonly known as the laws of war. 

Their primary purpose is to limit the barbarity of war by protecting people who are not actively participating in hostilities (such as civilians, aid workers, and medical personnel) and those who can no longer fight (such as wounded, sick, or shipwrecked soldiers, and prisoners of war).

---

## Rules Regarding POWs and Torture

The rules regarding Prisoners of War (POWs) are primarily outlined in the **Third Geneva Convention**, while the ban on torture is woven deeply throughout all four conventions. 

### 1. Core Rules for Prisoners of War (POWs)
To qualify for formal POW status, individuals generally must be members of the authorized armed forces of a party to the conflict or militias who follow certain rules (wearing a uniform/distinguishable sign, carrying arms openly, and obeying the laws of war). Under the Third Convention, they are entitled to strict protections:
* **Humane Treatment:** POWs must be treated humanely at all times. Any act causing death or seriously endangering the health of a prisoner is strictly forbidden. 
* **Protection from Harm and Insults:** They must be protected against acts of violence, intimidation, insults, and **public curiosity** (which is why parading POWs on television or social media for propaganda is a violation).
* **Interrogation Limits:** When questioned, a POW is only bound to give their **name, rank, date of birth, and army serial number**. 
* **No Coercion:** No physical or mental torture, nor any other form of coercion, may be inflicted on prisoners to secure information of any kind. Prisoners who refuse to answer may not be threatened or insulted.
* **Conditions of Detention:** Captors must provide POWs with adequate food, water, clothing, shelter, and medical care equivalent to what they give their own forces. They cannot be held in close confinement (cells) unless for disciplinary reasons; they are internally displaced, not criminals.
* **Repatriation:** POWs must be released and repatriated (sent home) without delay **after the cessation of active hostilities**.

### 2. The Absolute Prohibition on Torture
Under the Geneva Conventions, the ban on torture is **absolute and non-negotiable**. There are no exceptions—not even the "ticking time bomb" scenario or a state of supreme national emergency.
* **Grave Breaches:** Torture and "inhuman treatment" or "willfully causing great suffering" are classified as **Grave Breaches** of the Conventions, which legally constitute **war crimes**. Any country hosting someone accused of a grave breach is obligated to prosecute them or extradite them for trial.
* **Common Article 3:** This specific article appears in all four Geneva Conventions and serves as a "mini-treaty" baseline protection. It explicitly prohibits "violence to life and person, in particular murder of all kinds, mutilation, cruel treatment, and torture,"以及 "outrages upon personal dignity, in particular humiliating and degrading treatment."

## The Guantanamo Bay Connection

Following the September 11 attacks, the U.S. government established a detention camp at Guantanamo Bay, Cuba, creating a massive international legal debate regarding the Geneva Conventions.

* **The U.S. Government's Legal Argument:** The Bush administration argued that Al-Qaeda and Taliban detainees were not traditional soldiers representing a recognized sovereign nation. They labeled them **"unlawful enemy combatants"** and argued that because they didn't meet the criteria of the Third Geneva Convention, they were not entitled to POW status. By holding them on leased Cuban territory (Guantanamo), the administration initially argued they were outside the jurisdiction of U.S. federal courts and the Geneva Conventions altogether, which opened the door to "enhanced interrogation techniques" (which the international community and later U.S. reports recognized as torture).
* **The Supreme Court Turning Point (*Hamdan v. Rumsfeld*, 2006):** The legal loophole was definitively closed by the U.S. Supreme Court. The Court ruled that even if the conflict didn't fit the traditional definition of an international war between states, **Common Article 3 of the Geneva Conventions applies to all detainees in U.S. custody anywhere in the world.** > **The Impact:** This ruling meant that Guantanamo detainees were legally entitled to baseline humanitarian protections. It explicitly banned cruel treatment and torture, and ruled that the ad-hoc military commissions created to try them without standard judicial protections were illegal under both U.S. law and the Geneva Conventions.

## Does It Apply Domestically to a Country’s Own Citizens?

The application of the Geneva Conventions to a country's own citizens depends entirely on whether the country is in a state of **armed conflict**.

### During Peacetime (Does Not Apply)
No, the Geneva Conventions **do not apply** to standard domestic governance, civil protests, riots, or normal domestic law enforcement against a country's own citizens. If a government abuses its citizens during peacetime, it is violating **International Human Rights Law (IHRL)** (such as the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights) and its own domestic constitution—but it is not violating the Geneva Conventions.

### During a Civil War / Internal Armed Conflict (Does Apply)
Yes, if the situation escalates from civil unrest into a full-blown **Non-International Armed Conflict (NIAC)**—such as a civil war, armed insurgency, or revolution—**Common Article 3** is automatically triggered. 

Under Common Article 3, a government is legally bound to apply humanitarian rules to **its own citizens** who are part of the opposing insurgent forces, provided they have laid down their arms or are *hors de combat* (out of the fight due to sickness, wounds, or detention). In these scenarios, a state is strictly forbidden from torturing, mutilating, or summarily executing its own citizen-combatants without a trial by a regularly constituted court.
