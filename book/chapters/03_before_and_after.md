# Chapter 3 — Before and After

> Before being forcefully placed in Ocean Shores, Washington, I was free to roam about Aberdeen and Hoquiam anywhere I wanted to, filming everything. I would go on three to four walks a day, starting in early 2023. I was rebuilding my eco and my balance. I utilized the sound signals at traffic stops. The city was fairly appropriate to me. It allowed me to walk around, utilizing my phone as my visual device.
>
> I used to love going shopping at the Goodwill and at Wal-Mart nearly every day, buying dresses, socks, hats, purses. All of these establishments loved having me there! I integrated with most of them, even became partners and affiliates, and put them up on my websites.
>
> I miss going to Safeway and giving an employee named Miss Penny a quarter or two for every transaction I would make so that others would have grocery bags and never be without.
>
> I miss getting chicken at Safeway and having individuals randomly show up asking me for an autograph, and then walking over to the Petlin cemetery alone. I get to sit at the top and eat my chicken in peace and videotape the beauty of nature.
>
> By the end, I found it strange that I had become a minor celebrity within the area and people were traveling across the state and other states to meet me, but looking back now, I miss it. I had built up so many positive resources before being forcefully placed out here.

> From April of 2024 to November of 2024, I was able to get fresh food 3-4 times a day by walking and going and purchasing it and still having money left over and making money from the purchase itself. Being in Ocean Shores, Washington, I've been forced to eat out of a garbage can at McDonald's multiple times, the first time in my life.

> Every week to every two weeks, I would get my hair cut, my legs waxed, my face waxed, and my nails done at multiple different salons and nail places, but being out here, I haven't been allowed to.

> While being in Aberdeen and Hoquiam establishments loved me spending hundreds of dollars at each of them every month and bringing them views on social media. I got private tours to things such as the green room and musical events and more, but the moment I was forcefully placed out here in Ocean Shores, Washington, I found that the businesses didn't like a trans female inside of there. They treated me as uneducated, belittled me, short-changed me, grabbed my arms, put their hands on my shoulders and my back, searched through my purses, and talked down to me and called me "dude" and "man".

> In Ocean Shores, Washington, I've been hit in the head multiple times with rocks, hit by cars, threatened, and had attempted murder placed on me, but none of that happened in Aberdeen or Hoquiam.

> Prior to being forcefully placed in Ocean Shores, Washington, I built up so much resource: socially, financially, communally. I saved a lady. I saved a group of children. I got placed on a missed wraparound program that gave me an opportunity to move anywhere I wanted. All the arrangements were made for Seattle. Up until the moment I informed them that when I get to Seattle, I'll be seeking legal aid.

> I had built up six months' worth of free rent, move-in costs, security, and deposit and more for the good acts that I had produced and for the damage that they realized they were causing.

> Saving a person at that same animal shelter in April of 2023. I decided to no longer go back there to volunteer, but sometimes I've gone in. They give me too many things. They called me a hero too many times. They had the reflections of guilt on their face for not doing something more.

> I have found that when one is kidnapped, it's best to just give in to their kidnappers and attackers and to not struggle. It's going to happen anyway, and the resistance just hurts, attempting to try.
>
> I have found that it's best to be patient and to circumnavigate, to do what's told, and when they hit and strangle and beat down, to just give up. There's nothing one can do.
>
> I have found that obeying one's kidnappers may seem correct in the moment because it hurts less, but it adds up over time. The pain is immense.
